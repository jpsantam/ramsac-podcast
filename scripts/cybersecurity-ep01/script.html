BEAT 30:35–2:35The conversation
LouBig company, this one?
RobMid-sized. Nobody outside their industry's heard of them. Technically, nothing "happened" — the polite word for a hack you agreed to.
LouRight — let's deal with the obvious problem. You sell testing. So of course you're going to say everyone needs it.
RobGuilty as charged. Same logic as the fire brigade telling you to check your smoke alarm — annoying, but not wrong. Find nothing, you've paid for confidence instead of fear. Find something, it's on your terms, not a criminal's — a ranked list, not a panic: what's wrong, what it costs, what to fix first, with the budget you've got.
LouNo pitch. Just the pattern.
RobExactly why it's written that way.
LouSecond objection — I'll play skeptical board member. We're small. Nobody's coming for us.
RobBackwards, if anything. It's not about being interesting — it's about whether you run digital systems and hold data, and unless you're doing this from a cave, you do. It's also stopped being just criminals asking — insurers increasingly want evidence of testing before renewal, and bigger clients ask before they'll sign.
LouSo "optional" quietly became "due diligence."
BEAT 42:35–2:55What ramsac sees
LouNo pitch, just the pattern — so what are you actually finding out there?
RobAlmost every test, same short list: a reused password, a missed update, an old account nobody switched off. Nothing exotic. The businesses that improve aren't the ones with better tools — they're the ones who work through the list instead of filing it in a drawer and feeling better about themselves.
LouSo the danger's not the vulnerability. It's the drawer.
RobIt's always the drawer.
