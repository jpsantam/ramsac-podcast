You're a Target Whether You Know It or Not

Series opener. Establishes that ordinary technical gaps — not how newsworthy or high-profile a business is — are what make it exploitable. Sets the stakes the rest of the cybersecurity miniseries builds on.

Source "Ethical Hacking: What Does It Mean?" (ramsac.com/blog) Format Audio-only · target ~8 min Audience C-suite & IS/IT leaders Next episode EP02 — Teams impersonation & ClickFix

What changed in v10 — audio-first pivot

  • No more AI avatars; this is now an audio-only podcast. The HeyGen video demo landed well on script and tone, but the reviewer found the avatars distracting, off-format for how the audience actually listens, and a giveaway that the thing is AI-made. The content stands on its own without them.
  • The 3:15 cap is gone. That ceiling only ever existed to fit HeyGen's short video format — it was never a content call. Back to the plan's original 8–12 minute template, cut here to the leaner ~8-minute end of that band. The same praised content now has room to breathe: deeper objection handling, the "automated, not targeted" point drawn out, and a longer What ramsac sees segment.
  • Delivery fixes baked in. The two pauses the reviewer flagged are rewritten so the stop is motivated rather than stranded, and load-bearing lines carry inline emotion cues (below) so a flat read can't turn a beat like a dry "Fine" into an angry one.
  • Unchanged: the beats, the jokes, the objection/payoff structure, and content-policy compliance — no politics, no ramsac-specific numbers.

Production — ElevenLabs (audio-only)

Model: eleven_v3. Needed for the inline emotion tags (the dry-style cues in the script) that keep tone from drifting on delivery. If you drop to a model without tag support, strip the cues and re-check every flagged line by ear.

Voices — both British; audition and A/B before locking. Lou (Generalist, female): candidates Alice (confident, conversational) or Lily (warm) — the female read was already the natural one in the demo, so stay close to it. Rob (Specialist, male): first choice George (warm, mature); Daniel only if you raise Speed. The demo's male read slow and synthetic — fix with a naturally-paced voice, Stability preset Natural (not Robust), Speed ~1.05.

Pacing is punctuation. Comma = short breath, full stop = a beat, ellipsis … = trailing/hesitation, em-dash — = a quick self-interruption or cut, blank line between beats = a longer pause. Generate each host's lines on their own voice, then interleave in the edit — don't ask one render to switch speakers.

RobMid-sized. Nobody outside their own industry would know them. And technically? Nothing "happened." That's the polite word for a hack you agreed to in advance.
LouA test. A white-hat thing.
RobA test. Same skills as the criminal, opposite intent — and a report at the end instead of a ransom note.
LouOkay. Let's get the obvious objection out of the way, because I'd be thinking it. You sell this. So of course the man who sells testing says everyone needs testing.

Find something, and you found it on a Tuesday, on your terms — not at two in the morning from a criminal who's already copied everything.
LouSo it's not the scare-tape, red-writing report.
RobOpposite. It's a ranked list. What's wrong, what it would actually cost you, what to fix first — matched to the budget you've genuinely got, not the one in a fantasy. Boring. On purpose.
LouNo pitch. Just the pattern.
RobThat's the whole idea.
LouBefore we go further — practical one, because a board will ask it. Does this take our systems down? Are you unplugging things while everyone's trying to work?
RobNo. It's scoped and agreed up front — what's in bounds, what's off-limits, run quietly in the background and out of hours where it matters. Nobody walks in to find the website in bits. The whole point is that it's the rehearsal, not the fire.
LouSo it's the drill, not the drill sergeant.
RobamusedI'll allow that one.
LouAnd when you hand it over — do we just get a to-do list and a bill?
RobYou get a list your own IT can act on — or we do the hands-on part, if you'd rather. Either way it's ranked, not a data-dump: the few things that actually move your risk, first. Honestly, most of a good report is telling you what not to panic about.

LouAlright — second objection, and now I'm the skeptical board member, arms folded. We're small. We're not a bank. Who's actually coming for us?
RobThat's the one that gets people hurt, because it's backwards. Nobody's picking you off a list because you're fascinating. The attacks that hit small and mid-sized firms aren't chosen — they're automated. A program knocks on ten thousand doors an hour and strolls through whichever ones are unlocked. It never once asks who you are.
LouTen thousand doors an hour. So it's not somebody in a dark room picking us out.
RobIt's a script on rented machines, sweeping the whole internet for the same handful of mistakes, on a loop. You're not on a hit list. You're on a map — and the map draws itself.
LouSo the yardstick was never "are we a target."
RobThe yardstick is: do you run digital systems, and do you hold data somebody would rather you didn't lose. Unless you're doing all this from a cave — you do.
LouconcedingFine. warm, not sharpThat's — annoyingly hard to argue with.
RobAnd it's stopped being only the criminals asking. Your insurer wants evidence of testing before they'll renew the cover. Your bigger clients want it before they'll sign. Quietly, "optional" turned into "due diligence" — and most people find that out the week they need the cover, not the week before.
LouSo the deadline isn't the attacker. It's the renewal letter.
RobHalf the time, yes. The attack is hypothetical until it isn't. The renewal is on a date.
BEAT 45:45–7:00What ramsac sees
LouSo — no pitch, just the pattern. Across all these tests, what are you actually finding? The real list, not the brochure one.
RobYou'll be disappointed by how dull it is. Nearly every test, the same short list. A password somebody reused. An update nobody installed. An old account belonging to someone who left ages ago that nobody ever switched off.
LouNo zero-day super-weapon.
RobAlmost never. It's housekeeping. And here's the part that actually matters — the firms that get safer aren't the ones who spend the most, or buy the cleverest tool. They're the ones who take the list and work through it. The ones who don't… file it. Feel better for having done a test. Change nothing.
LouSo the danger was never really the vulnerability.
RobThe danger's the drawer. The report goes in the drawer, and the drawer is where good intentions go to die.
LouIt's always the drawer.
RobIt's always the drawer.
