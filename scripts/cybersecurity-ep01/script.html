You're a Target Whether You Know It or Not

Series opener. Establishes that ordinary technical gaps — not how newsworthy or high-profile a business is — are what make it exploitable. Sets the stakes the rest of the cybersecurity miniseries builds on.

Source "Ethical Hacking: What Does It Mean?" (ramsac.com/blog) Target runtime ~9:45–10:15 Audience C-suite & IS/IT leaders Next episode EP02 — Teams impersonation & ClickFix

BEAT 10:00–0:20Cold open
[No music, no branding — drops straight into the scene]
Specialist A security tester logs on for a client's first authorised test of the year. Eighteen minutes in, they're already inside the network — not because of some brilliant piece of hacking, but because one admin account was still using the password it shipped with. The report that goes out on Monday has a single line at the top: "time to initial access: eighteen minutes."
[Beat of silence. Stinger.]
BEAT 20:20–0:45Intro
GeneralistWelcome to The Ten-Minute Brief from ramsac — ten minutes, once a week, on the technology stuff that actually affects how you run your business. Episode one of a five-part run on cybersecurity, and we're opening with eighteen minutes, apparently, so —
SpecialistI did warn you it wasn't going to be a gentle start.
GeneralistYou did. I'm the one asking the questions on behalf of anyone with a business — or a security budget — to run.
SpecialistAnd I'm the one who already spends too much time in this world. Let's get into it.
BEAT 30:45–7:45The conversation
GeneralistOkay, eighteen minutes — was that a big company? Because that's the assumption a lot of boards still make. The famous ones are the targets.
SpecialistMid-sized. Nobody outside their own industry has ever heard of them. And technically nothing even happened — that was a penetration test.
GeneralistEthical hacking — I'll play the skeptical board member for a second. White hat, black hat, I get the concept. What I actually want to know is what the window was.
SpecialistNever dramatic, that's the thing. A password that got reused. A server that missed a patch. Someone's login nobody switched off after they changed roles. A file sitting public when it shouldn't be.
GeneralistSo nothing that needed nation-state budget to find.
SpecialistNothing. It's someone who knows where people forget to look, not someone brilliant.
GeneralistAlright, now I'm playing CFO. You're a company that sells this. "You need testing, and conveniently, we sell testing." Come on.
SpecialistCompletely fair. Same energy as the fire brigade telling you to check your smoke alarm — annoying that they'd benefit, doesn't make them wrong. The incentive doesn't change whether the test's actually true. Either we find something or we don't.
GeneralistAnd if you don't?
SpecialistYou've paid for confidence instead of fear. Fine thing to buy.
GeneralistAnd if you do.
SpecialistYou were always going to find out. This way it's on your terms instead of a criminal's — and you get it as a ranked list, not a panic. Here's what's wrong, here's what it costs to be wrong about it, here's what to fix first with the budget you've actually got. Not "you have a problem."
GeneralistThat's the bit that actually lands on a CFO's desk, not just IT's.
SpecialistThat's exactly why it's written that way.
GeneralistNo pitch, just the pattern. I'm holding you to that for the rest of this series.
SpecialistPlease do.
GeneralistWe ran one of those free online scanners last year. Came back clean. Isn't that basically this, for free?
SpecialistDifferent job. That scanner never actually tried anything — it checks for known issues automatically. It won't chain two small weaknesses together, or send a fake phishing email and see who clicks. A test is a person thinking like an attacker.
GeneralistSo "clean scan" was never "we know where we stand."
SpecialistNot even close.
GeneralistFine — but "too small to be a target" surely still holds under, say, five hundred seats.
SpecialistBackwards, if anything. It was never about being interesting — it's whether you run on digital systems and hold data. Below that five-hundred mark you usually have fewer of these gaps closed, not more. Less dedicated security headcount, systems that grew organically instead of being designed.
GeneralistAnd it's not just the criminals asking the question now, is it.
SpecialistNo — insurers are increasingly asking for evidence of testing at renewal, and plenty of your bigger clients ask before they'll sign a contract at all.
GeneralistSo it's stopped being optional even for people who'd rather it stayed optional.
SpecialistFor a lot of our clients, yes.
GeneralistSo practically — once a year, tick the box, done?
SpecialistOnce a year, or after anything big changes — new systems, a merger, a big staff shift. And someone actually working through the report afterwards. A ranked list sitting unread in an inbox protects nobody, however good the report is.
GeneralistA habit, not a January box-tick.
SpecialistThat's the whole idea. The point was never to be unhackable. Nobody is. It's knowing where you stand on purpose, instead of finding out by accident — and being able to show your board, or your insurer, that you did.
BEAT 47:45–9:00What ramsac sees
GeneralistAlright, this is the bit where you tell me something you're actually seeing out there. No pitch, just the pattern — like we agreed.
SpecialistJust the pattern. The single most common finding, across almost every test we run, isn't some clever new technique. It's one of a small handful of basics — a reused password, a missed update, access that never got switched off.
GeneralistBoringly consistent.
SpecialistPainfully. And what actually separates organisations isn't the finding, it's what happens to the report afterwards. The ones who improve fastest aren't buying more tools. They're working through the ranked list in order, instead of filing it as done for the year.
GeneralistSo the pattern isn't the vulnerability. It's the follow-through.
SpecialistEvery time.
BEAT 59:00–9:45Recap
GeneralistOne sentence, for anyone who's already forgotten the eighteen-minute story: you don't have to be newsworthy to be exploitable, you just need an unlocked window, and a proper test is how you find it — ranked, budgeted, and on your terms — before someone else does.
SpecialistAnd next time we're staying close to home in a different way — the next attacks aren't a tester poking your network from outside. They're hiding inside Teams and your browser, in tools your staff already trust completely.
GeneralistGood. Looking forward to being uncomfortable.
BEAT 69:45–10:15Soft CTA
SpecialistIf you've never actually had someone test where your gaps are, or it's been a while, ramsac's happy to talk it through. No pitch. Just the pattern.
GeneralistJust the pattern. Thanks for listening.
